03

About the Plant

Ginger is a widely used medicinal and culinary plant known for its underground stem called a rhizome, which has a strong flavour and powerful medicinal properties. The plant grows about 1 metre tall and has long, narrow green leaves with yellowish-green flowers.

Ginger is rich in bioactive compounds such as gingerol, shogaol, and zingerone, which give the plant its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anticancer properties, making it one of the most valued plants in both traditional and modern medicine.

04

Historical Background

Ginger has been used in traditional medicine for more than 3000 years. It was widely used in Ayurvedic, Chinese, and traditional medicine systems for treating digestive problems, colds, and inflammation. Ancient traders spread ginger from Asia to Europe and the Middle East, where it became an important medicinal and spice plant.

Historical texts from India, China, and Arabia all document ginger's use as a therapeutic agent, cementing its place as one of the most globally recognised medicinal plants throughout human history.

05

Origin and Distribution

Ginger is believed to have originated in Southeast Asia, particularly in regions of India and China. Today it is widely cultivated in India, China, Indonesia, Thailand, and tropical regions worldwide. India is one of the largest producers of ginger globally. The plant grows best in warm, humid climates with well-drained soil.

06

Plant Parts Used for Oral Cancer

The main part of ginger used for oral cancer research is the rhizome (ginger root). It contains important bioactive compounds with documented anticancer activity:

  • Rhizome — primary source of all medicinal compounds; used as extract, powder, or oil
  • Gingerol — the principal active compound; induces apoptosis in cancer cells
  • Shogaol — formed from gingerol on drying; shows potent anticancer effects
  • Paradol — exhibits anti-proliferative and antioxidant activity
  • Zingerone — contributes anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties
07

Anticancer Mechanism

Apoptosis Induction
Gingerol triggers programmed death of oral cancer cells, halting tumour progression.
Antioxidant Activity
Neutralises free radicals that cause DNA damage and promote cancerous mutations.
Anti-inflammatory
Reduces chronic oral inflammation associated with tumour development and progression.
Tumour Inhibition
Prevents proliferation of oral cancer cells through multiple signalling pathways.
Anti-metastasis
Helps inhibit the spread of cancer cells to surrounding and distant tissues.
08

Traditional Uses

  • Treatment of nausea, vomiting, and digestive disorders
  • Relief from cold, cough, and sore throat — commonly taken with honey or warm water
  • Used for reducing inflammation and pain in joints and muscles
  • Improves digestion and speeds up metabolism
  • Used in herbal medicines and Ayurvedic preparations
  • Widely used as a spice and flavouring in food and beverages worldwide

Ginger is considered an important medicinal plant due to its health benefits, therapeutic properties, and potential role in cancer prevention.
