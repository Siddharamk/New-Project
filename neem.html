03

About the Plant

Neem is a fast-growing evergreen tree native to the Indian subcontinent, capable of reaching heights of 15–20 metres. It is one of the most researched plants in the world due to its extraordinary range of biological activities. The tree is recognised by its spreading branches, dark green compound leaves, and small white fragrant flowers. Every part of the neem tree — leaves, bark, seeds, roots, and flowers — possesses medicinal value.

Neem is a cornerstone of Ayurvedic medicine, prized for its antifungal, antibacterial, antiviral, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer properties. It plays an essential role in traditional oral health practices across South Asia.

04

Historical Background

Neem has been used in Ayurvedic medicine for over 4000 years. Ancient Sanskrit texts refer to neem as "Sarva Roga Nivarini" — the curer of all ailments. The Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita, two of the oldest Ayurvedic texts, extensively document its use in treating skin diseases, fevers, infections, and dental disorders.

Neem twigs have traditionally been used as natural toothbrushes across India and Africa, a practice now validated by modern dentistry research confirming their antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties.

05

Origin and Distribution

Neem originated in the Indian subcontinent and Myanmar. Today it is widely distributed across tropical and subtropical regions including India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Africa, and parts of the Americas. It grows well in semi-arid conditions, tolerates poor soil quality, and is commonly found along roadsides, in villages, and in agricultural settings across India.

06

Plant Parts Used for Oral Cancer

Several parts of the neem plant are used in oral cancer research and treatment:

  • Leaves — most commonly used; rich in nimbolide, quercetin, and flavonoids
  • Bark — used in decoctions for oral hygiene and anti-inflammatory effects
  • Seeds / Seed oil — contain azadirachtin and nimbin with cytotoxic activity
  • Flowers — used in traditional preparations for mouth ulcers and lesions
  • Roots — studied for antimicrobial and immunomodulatory compounds
07

Anticancer Mechanism

Antioxidant Activity
Neutralises free radicals that damage DNA and trigger cancerous mutations in oral cells.
Apoptosis Induction
Nimbolide and nimbin trigger programmed cell death specifically in oral cancer cell lines.
Anti-inflammatory
Reduces chronic oral inflammation, a key driver of cancer progression in mucosal tissues.
Anti-angiogenesis
Inhibits formation of new blood vessels that supply nutrients to tumour tissue.
Cell Cycle Arrest
Neem compounds halt cancer cell proliferation at key phases of the cell cycle.
Immunomodulation
Enhances the immune system's recognition and elimination of tumour cells.
08

Traditional Uses

  • Neem leaf extract mouthwash for reducing oral inflammation and treating ulcers
  • Neem oil applied topically to oral lesions and wounds
  • Neem bark decoction used daily for improved oral hygiene and gum health
  • Neem twig (datun) used as a natural toothbrush across rural India
  • Neem capsules and churna taken internally for systemic anticancer and detox effects
  • Used in Ayurvedic formulations for treating skin disorders, fever, and infections
09

Research Findings

Modern scientific research has validated the anticancer properties of neem compounds. Key findings include:

  • Nimbolide inhibits proliferation and induces apoptosis in oral squamous carcinoma cell lines
  • Neem leaf extract significantly reduces tumour volume in in vivo oral cancer models
  • Azadirachtin demonstrates selective cytotoxicity against cancer cells while sparing normal cells
  • Neem compounds enhance the effectiveness of conventional chemotherapy drugs
  • Anti-angiogenic activity of neem has been confirmed in multiple laboratory studies
